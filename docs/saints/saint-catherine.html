Saint Catherine of Alexandria

† 305 CE (traditional)

Feast day: 25 November

Virgin, martyr, and philosopher of Alexandria. One of the most frequently depicted saints in medieval art; patron of scholars and students.

Biography

Catherine is presented by the hagiographic tradition as a young noblewoman of exceptional intellect who lived in Alexandria under the emperor Maxentius. Versed in all the sciences of her day, she defended the Christian faith against fifty pagan philosophers summoned to persuade her to abjure: rather than being persuaded, she converted her adversaries.

Condemned to the wheel fitted with iron blades, she was miraculously delivered by an angel who shattered the instrument of torture. She was ultimately beheaded, and according to legend milk rather than blood flowed from her wound. Angels carried her body to Mount Sinai, where her cult grew around the monastery that today bears her name.

In the Middle Ages, Catherine was among the Fourteen Holy Helpers and one of the voices heard by Joan of Arc. Her Life, composed in several Latin versions, was translated and adapted into many vernacular languages, including several texts in Old French and Middle English of great literary quality — among them the Life of Saint Catherine composed by Clemence of Barking in the twelfth century.
