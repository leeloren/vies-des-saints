Saint Nicholas of Myra

c. 270–343 CE

Feast day: 6 December

Bishop of Myra in Lycia, miracle-worker, and patron of children, sailors, and students. One of the most popular figures in the medieval Western calendar.

Biography

Nicholas was born around 270 in Patara, Lycia (modern southern Turkey), into a prosperous family that provided him with a thorough Christian education. Left an orphan at an early age, he distributed his inheritance to the poor, fulfilling the Gospel injunction to the letter. Elected Bishop of Myra while still a young monk, he attended the Council of Nicaea in 325.

The medieval tradition attributes numerous miracles to him, the most celebrated being the resurrection of three young students whose host had killed and pickled them in a barrel, and the secret dowry provided for three daughters of a ruined man — the gesture behind the iconography of the three golden balls. His tomb at Myra became a pilgrimage site, and his relics were translated to Bari in 1087, giving rise to the Basilica of Saint Nicholas and a new center of his cult in the West.

In France, the cult of Saint Nicholas was extraordinarily popular, especially in Lorraine, where the Jeu de saint Nicolas by Jean Bodel (late 12th–early 13th century) is one of the earliest dramatic miracles in Old French. His verse Life was composed by numerous anonymous authors and by named writers such as Wace.
